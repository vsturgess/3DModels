Understanding Oxygen Perfusion Within the Myocardium

Capillary Network Alignment

Oxygen and nutrients are delivered to tissue beds via networks of small capillary vessels. In the myocardium, capillaries run parallel to the direction of muscle fibers. Interconnections between parallel capillaries occur as a result of branching patterns, including "cross-connecting" capillaries.

Idealized Capillary Network

The geometry used in this model has one arterial inlet, two venous outlets, and a capillary network. The capillary network is evenly spaced around cylindrical cardiomyocytes. Blood enters the network through the arterial inlet and leaves through two venous outlets. The placement of these inlets and outlets can be observed in the interactive model.

Capillary Permeability

Capillary walls are a thin layer of cells that allow nutrients and gases such as oxygen to pass between the blood vessel and the surrounding tissue. Permeation of solutes (dissolved material) across a membrane depends on the concentration of the solute on either side of the membrane.

In physiology, the measurement of dissolved oxygen and other dissolved gases is typically converted to a partial pressure. For example, the partial pressure of oxygen is the concentration of oxygen multiplied by the solubility coefficient.

Partial pressure of oxygen may also be written as PO2.

Oxygen in the Blood

Dissolved oxygen has a limited solubility in aqueous environments. One of the primary roles of hemoglobin in red blood cells is to carry additional oxygen molecules and increase the overall oxygen concentration in blood.

Oxygen-hemoglobin saturation (or Hb saturation) defines the percentage of oxygen binding sites on hemoglobin that are carrying an oxygen molecule. Partial pressure of oxygen (PO2) and oxygen hemoglobin saturation are related such that as the concentration of dissolved oxygen decreases, oxygen dissociates from hemoglobin, and oxygen-hemoglobin saturation decreases.
